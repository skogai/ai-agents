# Survivorship Bias

## Principle

Survivorship bias occurs when we draw conclusions from winners while ignoring the losers who used the same strategy. The visible survivors distort our understanding of what actually works.

## Classic Example

During WWII, Abraham Wald studied bullet holes on returning bombers. The military wanted to armor the areas with the most damage. Wald argued the opposite: armor the areas with no damage, because planes hit there never returned.

## Decision Critic Application

Use this model during **Decomposition** (Steps 1-2) and **Challenge** (Steps 5-6) to surface hidden data gaps.

### Verification Questions

When a decision cites evidence of success:

1. What failures used the same approach but are not visible?
2. Are we studying survivors or the full population?
3. What data are we missing because failures were discarded?
4. Is the sample self-selected (only successes reported)?

### Red Flags in Decisions

| Signal | Risk |
|--------|------|
| "Company X did this and succeeded" | Ignoring companies that did the same and failed |
| "Our successful projects all used pattern Y" | Failed projects using Y were forgotten |
| "This approach always works" | Selection bias in evidence |
| "Top performers do Z" | Studying survivors, not the full cohort |
| "We've never had a problem with this" | Problems were silently discarded or unreported |

### Contrarian Perspectives (Step 5)

Generate these challenges when survivorship bias is suspected:

- "How many teams tried this approach and failed? Where is that data?"
- "Are we looking at the planes that came back, or the ones that didn't?"
- "Is our evidence base self-selected?"

### Failure Modes (for Inversion Analysis)

- Adopting a strategy because visible successes used it, ignoring invisible failures
- Reinforcing existing patterns because only their successes are measured
- Dismissing alternatives because no visible successes exist (they were never tried)
- Over-investing in approaches with high visibility but unknown failure rates

## Practical Checklist

Before accepting evidence-based claims as VERIFIED:

- [ ] Failure cases examined, not just successes
- [ ] Sample includes non-survivors (full population)
- [ ] Selection mechanism for evidence is understood
- [ ] Base rate of success/failure is known or estimated
- [ ] Alternative explanations for observed pattern considered

## Related Models

- Confirmation Bias: seeking evidence that supports existing beliefs
- Base Rate Neglect: ignoring overall probability when evaluating specific cases
- Systems Thinking: examining the full system, not just visible outputs
